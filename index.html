Introduction to Coral Reefs

Why Study the Ocean?

  • Approximately 70% of Earth is covered by ocean.
    The Earth's surface is 71% water and 29% land. Of that 71%, 97% is found in the oceans. The remaining 3% is either locked up in the ice caps and glaciers, or is found in fresh water bodies.
  • 230 thousand species have been recorded in the sea. There may be an estimated 1-10 million in total!
    A huge number of species and taxonomic groups live in the sea. It’s thought the majority of which could be undiscovered, but are nonetheless important for ecological functioning and could hold the key to important scientific breakthroughs.
  • 10-12% of humans depend on fishing and aquaculture for income.
    Aquaculture is the harvesting of marine resources, e.g. seaweed or shellfish.
  • Many products (e.g. medicine) come from the sea.
    Coral reefs in particular have very complex ways of protecting themselves from the reef, so there is a lot of interest in them from the medical industry.
  • We have only explored around 5% of the oceans.
    As oceans make up most of the Earth’s crust, this means that most of the world remains unexplored and therefore a mystery!
  • The marine ecosystem connects with the land ecosystem to form a 'global ecosystem'.
    The world is a highly connected place. What happens in the ocean effects what happens on the land. For example, fish get eaten by birds. Birds get eaten by land carnivores.
  • ...And because it is UNDER THREAT.
    The oceans are under assault from an array of anthropogenic disturbances. These include climate change, overfishing, ocean acidification and plastic pollution. There has never been a more important time to support marine conservation!

What is a Reef?

A reef is a structure that provides relief on the seafloor and a barrier to water movement. Reefs were relevant to sailors long before they were studied by scientists as they had to be careful to navigate them successfully. The term comes from the norse term 'rif' which means hazardous 'rib' close to the surface of the sea. Reefs can be...

natural (e.g.)...

  • Coral
  • Rock
  • Calcified algae
or artificial (e.g.)...
  • Shipwreck
  • Cement blocks
The majority of sea life can't survive for too long on an open sand back because they are vulnerable to getting swept about or being easily eatern by predators.

What is Coral?

Coral is made of a colony of animal components called polyps. We group corals into two types. Hard corals and soft corals. Hard corals are responsible for establishing long-lasting reef structures. They have hard calcium carbonate skeletons that stay preserved after the polyps die. This enables new generations to build on top of dead ones, meaning large reef structures can build up over many years.

Corals also contain zooxanthellae. This is a type of tiny photosynthetic algae that makes food and therefore energy that gets used by the coral.

Where Do (Most) Corals Thrive?

We can divide the oceans into three latitudinal zones.

  • Tropical Seas
    The water temperature is warm - over 18 degrees celsius. There is little fluctuation of conditions making it very stable for life.
  • Temperate Seas
    Temperatures range from 10 to 18 degrees celsius. These regions are highly seasonal. In the winter months, the seas are often rough. This increases the mixing of nutrients that causes blooms of plankton at certain times of year.
  • Polar Seas
    The waters here are under 10 degrees celsius. Ice often forms on the ocean surface. Animals living here need adaptations to prevent their bodies from feeding.

    • Photosynthetic coral reefs can only thrive in certain conditions. They need...

    • Warm temperatues all year round.
      Their optimum temperature ranges from 23 to 29 degrees celsius.
    • A lot of sunlight
      This is because corals get their energy from tiny photosynthetic organisms that grow on them called zooxanthellae. This process involves the use of sunlight to produce food for the coral. For this reason, coral reefs are generally restricted to the top 60 meters of the sea.
    • 'Oligotrophic' waters
      This means waters with low nutrient levels. This may seem surprising. The reason is because coral is rather slow growing. If there is a high level of nutrients in the water, it will get quickly consumed by algae (a.k.a. seaweed) and this will grow rapidly outgrow and overtake the coral. It will take up space, blockout sunlight and use up much of the oxygen. All these factors will reduce the health and growth of coral.

    Because of these requirements, photosynthetic coral reefs are restricted to shallow tropical waters, generally within 30 degrees north or south of the equator.

    There are exceptions however. There are deep sea and cold water corals that can sometimes be found as deep as over 2,000m. They do not recieve sunlight and therefore have no zooxanthellae. They feed by catching small particles of organic matter falling from shallower water. The communities of organisms that deep sea reefs harbour are very different to those of tropical phosotynthetic reefs.

    Reef Community Hubs

    There are three 'hotspots' of tropical reef communities. They are the Caribbean, the Red Sea and the Indo-Pacific. The most biodiverse reefs in the world are found in the Indo-Pacific. This is due to a number of factors. For one thing, it is fed by biological communities from multiple oceans and land masses. The reefs here therefore recieve a lot of geographical overlap. Additionally, the seas here recieve an optimum amount of disturbance. The Caribbean recieves stronger and more severe storms, and this can inhibit the flourishing of wildlife. In contrast, the Red Sea receives very little rough weather. Surprisingly, this can also lower the diversity because a certain amount of stormy weather can help to restore the balance of nature by preventing certain species from dominating at the expense of others.

Importance for Nature

Coral reefs contain the most biodiversity of any ecosystem. They occupy less than 1% of the sea floor, but contain around one quarter of all sea life. This is due to the structural support provided by the coral itself. This acts as good shelter for fish and other organisms to thrive in. It also provides good anchorage for other organisms to grow on top of. This provides food for the fish. As the population of fish thrive, this brings in predators that eat the fish. The result is a rich flourising ecosystem. The animal kingdom is grouped into 36 phyla. Nine can be found in tropical rainforests. Coral reefs harbor 32. Coral reefs also connect with other ecosystems. Some species migrate into and out of a coral reef for feeding purposes or cleaning services. This means that if the reef suffers, these animals may perish and the other habitats that they travel to will also be affected. Additionally, a lot of the biomass from coral reefs trickle down to feed life in deeper parts of the ocean where food is more scarce.

Services for Humans

  • Food
    Coral reefs are a major food source for up to one billion people. (Estimates vary according to sources.) Approximately 6 million people directly catch fish on reefs.

  • Tourism
    Tourism around reefs comes in the form of diving, snorkelling, beach generation, sea food and more. Tourism from coral reefs is estimated to produce around $36 billion globally per year.

  • Coastal Protection
    Coral reefs act as a very important barrier against waves and erosion. Reefs dissipate up to 97% of wave energy. This is significant because without them, coastal erosion and destruction, including damage to human structures on the coast, would be high. It is estimated that the presence of reefs saves the global economy from more than $4 billion worth of damage from coastal erosion per year.

  • Other Resources
    Reefs also harbour other substances that are important or luxurious for humans. These include medicines, stock for the aquarium trade and building resources.

  • Cultural significance for many communities

All in all, coral reefs are estimated to produce around $30 billion per year for the global economy!

Connected Coastal Ecosystems

You cannot truely understand how coral reefs function without familiarising with neighbouring coastal systems that are very closely linked.

Mangrove Forests

Mangroves are trees that live partially submerged in saltwater. They have to be very specially adapted to deal with the stressors of tidal fluctuations, exposure to high salt concentration, anoxic sediment and high heat and ultraviolet intensity.

Seagrass Beds

Sea grass is the only true plant group (or 'angiosperm') that can live fully submerged in salt water. Unlike mangroves, which live exlusively in the tropics and sub-tropics, sea grass beds can be found in both tropical and temperatre latitudes. However, in the tropics they harbour significantly richer biological communities.


A typical undisturbed tropical coastal ecosystem will be commonly be structured with mangroves lining the coast, leading into a seagrass bed in the shallows, eventually transitioning into a coral reef. This is significant because there is a huge amount of ecological connectivity between the three habitats. In fact, about 80% of reef fish migrate to mangroves at some point in their life cycle. When mangroves or seagrass beds are destroyed, there is a huge knock-on effect on the health of neighbouring coral reefs. But why?...

The Importance of Mangroves and Seagrass Beds

Fish Nurseries
Mangroves, and to some extent sea grass beds, serve as nurseries for juvenile fish. They can start their life in a refuge where it is hard for larger predators to fit between the dense network of roots. Once they grow larger and less vulnerable they then migrate onto the reef.

Coastal Protection
Mangroves provide a great deal of coastal protection from storm damage. After the 2004 Boxing Day tsunami in the Indo-Pacific, surveys demonstrated that locations that still had mangrove forests present had far less infrastructural ddamage than areas that had lost their mangroves to deforestation.

Sediment Stabilisation
Mangroves and seagrass thrive in areas where the sediment is highly fine and silty. This kind of sediment easily wafts about in the water. Coral does not thrive well in waters with high sediment as it can reduce sunlight and smother their feeding appendages. Mangroves and seagrass both have extensive roots that help to lock-in this sediment to prevent it from running onto the reef. When mangroves are deforested, one of the side-effects is an increase in turbidity (i.e. particulate matter in the water) on coral reefs.

Blue Carbon
Both habitats are an excellent store of what is referred to as 'blue carbon'. This is a modern term used to describe carbon storage associated with a marine environment. A meter squared of mangrove forest is actually a more efficient carbon sink than a meter squared of tropical rainforest.

Water Oxygenation
As both mangroves and seagrass carry out photosynthesis, they contribute towards oxygenation in the water. This is crucial to marine life as almost all living things require oxygen for respiration.

Nutrient Sink
Coral reefs need water with low nutrient levels so that they are not outcompeted by algae. Many of the nutrients in the sea has leached from the land. Where mangroves and seagrass are present, they mitigate the nutrient run-off from the land by absorbing a large amount of it before it reaches the reef.

Habitat for Many Species
Mangroves and seagrass beds are habitats for many species in their own right. This includes a number of charismatic marine species, such as manatees which feed on the grass, and terrestrial species such as birds, bugs and reptiles. They therefore serve as a link between the marine ecosystem and the terrestrial ecosystem, thus enhancing connectivity and flow within the 'global ecosystem'.
